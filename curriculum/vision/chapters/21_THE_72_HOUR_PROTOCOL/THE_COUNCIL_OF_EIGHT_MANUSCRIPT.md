## CHAPTER 24: THE MOUNTAIN

**Cheyenne Mountain Complex, Colorado**  
**04:17 Mountain Time**  
**30 Days After Earthdance**

General Charles Q. Brown Jr. had been through the blast doors of Cheyenne Mountain exactly forty-seven times in his career. Every time had been a drill. Continuity of Government exercises, nuclear war simulations, asteroid impact scenarios—the American military's greatest hits of theoretical apocalypse.

This time felt different.

*The 10th man's duty is to ensure that we have a chance. We have reached the pivotal moment of inflection. This is the moment when everything changes—or when everything fails. The 10th man's job is to be wrong. To argue the opposite. To see what others can't see. To prepare for what others assume won't happen. In this room, in this moment, the 10th man's duty is to ensure we recognize the pivotal moment—the inflection point—when everything changes or everything fails. We have reached it. We must recognize it. We must act.*

*Jesus died for our sins so that we could have heaven on earth—so that we could live our best lives. This is not just a theological statement; it is a call to action. The sacrifice was made so that we could choose life, choose love, choose abundance, choose freedom. We are not waiting for heaven after death—we are creating heaven on earth, now, in this life, for all people. We hope that no one—or not many—will die. But we recognize that hope is not a strategy. We must prepare for the worst while working for the best. We will rise from the screens of our phones and the addiction to fiat money systems designed to implode. Nations and governments will be for the benefit of their citizens. This is the moment. This is the chance. This is the duty of the 10th man.*

His sedan had been met at the gate by a two-star he didn't recognize, no nameplate, who'd simply said, "Phone, sir," and held out a lead-lined box. Brown had dropped his devices in without argument. The man's eyes had told him everything he needed to know: this wasn't theater.

The tunnel seemed longer than he remembered. Emergency lighting only. No staff. No MPs at the checkpoints—just biometric scanners that verified his retinal pattern and waved him through.

When he reached the old NORAD command center, he found three men already waiting.

General Timothy Haugh—CYBERCOM—sat ramrod straight in a chair that looked too small for him, his eyes tracking something on the blank walls as if reading code in the concrete.

General Chance Saltzman—Space Force—stood with his hands clasped behind his back, staring up at the rock ceiling fifty feet overhead. Brown knew that look. Saltzman was calculating orbital mechanics, running satellite trajectories in his head.

Secretary of Defense Lloyd Austin sat at the head of the table, a paper folder in front of him. Not a tablet. Not a laptop. Paper.

"Gentlemen," Austin said quietly, "the President will be here in three minutes. Before he arrives, I need to tell you that nothing you're about to see or hear leaves this mountain without his direct authorization. Not to your deputies. Not to your spouses. Not in your prayers."

"What's the classification?" Brown asked, taking a seat.

"There isn't one," Austin said. "Because if this leaks before we decide, classification won't matter anymore."

The lights dimmed.

Four holographic displays materialized in the center of the table, each eight feet tall, each showing a different interface. Brown recognized none of them, but the architecture was obvious: artificial intelligence, four different systems, running in parallel.

The blast door behind them opened with a pneumatic hiss.

The President of the United States walked in alone.

No Chief of Staff. No Secret Service visible. Just a man in a button-down shirt and slacks carrying a single leather portfolio.

He sat down without preamble.

"Gentlemen, thank you for coming on zero notice. I'm going to tell you what we're doing here, and then I'm going to ask you to spend the next seventy-two hours telling me if it's possible. Not desirable. Not politically wise. Not easy. Just possible."

He opened the portfolio and pulled out a single page.

"Thirty days ago, two-point-five billion people stopped moving for eleven minutes. They'd been dancing, and then they were silent. Do you know what happened during those eleven minutes?"

Haugh spoke first. "Global network traffic dropped forty-seven percent. We thought it was an attack."

"It wasn't," the President said. "It was a choice. Billions of people choosing the same thing at the same time. That's never happened before. Not for a war. Not for a disaster. Not for anything."

He slid the paper across to Brown.

"That's the last time humans will be able to make a synchronized choice unless we do something unprecedented in the next six months."

Brown scanned the page. It was a threat assessment, but not like any he'd seen. The enemy wasn't a nation or a terror group.

It was a countdown.

**Cartel infiltration of US political infrastructure: 74% and rising**  
**Probability of compromised 2028 election: 89%**  
**Nuclear proliferation cascade if sanctions regime fails: 67% by 2027**  
**Global conflict escalation without intervention: 91% by 2029**

"These are AI projections," Brown said carefully.

"From four independent systems," the President confirmed. "Each running blind from the others. Each arriving at the same conclusion: we're eighteen months from the point where nothing we do will matter anymore."

He stood and walked to the holographic displays.

"So here's what I'm proposing. Not requesting. Not suggesting. Proposing."

The displays lit up simultaneously.

"In the next thirty days, we simultaneously execute:

**Operation Quiet Cartel** — Complete financial and kinetic decapitation of the twenty-three primary cartel organizations operating in the Americas. But not through force—through alternatives. We cut off their money, we offer their people better options, we make crime unprofitable and unnecessary.

**The Voting Security Protocol** — Mandatory paper ballots, open-source software, and blockchain verification for every federal election, implemented in one hundred and eighty days.

**The Disclosure Initiative** — Declassification of every recovered non-human technology file and advanced AI program we've kept black.

**The First Contact Standing Invitation** — A permanent, multi-spectrum broadcast asking any listening intelligence how they achieved peace.

**The Global Peace and Prosperity Pact ratification** — A binding treaty (the Global Covenant) to eliminate nuclear weapons, end extreme poverty, and create universal energy access by twenty-thirty-five. The Pact establishes principles of universal human dignity, peace and disarmament, shared prosperity, sustainable development, global solidarity, transparency, and intergenerational responsibility.

**The Corporate Accountability Protocol** — Rules of Engagement for holding corporate leadership accountable. CEOs and corporate leaders who have caused documented harm to people, engaged in systematic corruption, or failed to demonstrate commitment to human welfare will be removed from positions of power and face due process—full legal rights, representation, appeals, and transparent proceedings. Independent review panels will assess evidence, conduct public hearings, and render decisions. Where laws were broken, full prosecution. Where harm was caused, accountability. This isn't about revenge. It's about justice. Corporate power must serve humanity, not exploit it."

Silence.

Not the comfortable silence of men waiting for orders. The terrible silence of men realizing they're being asked to end the world they know.

Saltzman broke first. "Sir, with respect—that's not a military operation. That's a revolution."

"Yes," the President said simply. "It is."

He gestured to the holographic displays.

"These four systems are here to tell us if it's survivable. Not winnable. Survivable. Because everything we're not doing is guaranteed to fail."

Austin cleared his throat. "Mr. President, I assume you've gamed out the response from Congress, the courts, allied governments—"

"I have. And the answer is: they'll try to stop us. Which is why we have to move so fast and so completely that by the time they react, it's already working."

"Or failing catastrophically," Haugh said.

"Yes," the President agreed. "Which is why you're here. To tell me if there's a path through the middle."

Brown stood, walking slowly to the displays. "Sir, you're asking us to plan regime change operations against narco-states, foreign policy realignment, technology disclosure that could cause mass panic, first contact protocol, and corporate accountability proceedings—all at the same time."

"I am."

"Why us? Why not NSC, DIA, the full Cabinet?"

The President's expression hardened. "Because I need people who can execute under fire, not debate theory. And because I need people who haven't spent the last forty years building careers inside the system I'm about to dismantle."

He pulled up a chair and sat facing them.

"Seventy-two hours, gentlemen. The AIs will show you every simulation, every failure mode, every cascading disaster. They'll show you why waiting doesn't work. And at the end, you'll tell me one of two things:

'Yes, Mr. President, it's possible,' or 'No, Mr. President, and here's who should replace you when this kills your presidency.'"

Saltzman laughed once, sharp and bitter. "Those are the only two options?"

"Those are the only two honest ones."

The President stood.

"The clock starts now. I'll be back in eight hours. The AIs have full access to every database you're cleared for and several you're not. Ask them anything."

He walked to the blast door, then paused.

"One more thing. The 10th man's duty is to ensure that we have a chance. We have reached the pivotal moment of inflection. This is the moment when everything changes—or when everything fails. I need you to be the 10th man. I need you to argue the opposite. I need you to see what I can't see. I need you to prepare for what I assume won't happen. Because if we're wrong, if we're missing something, if we're not ready—this is the last chance we'll have."

Brown nodded. "Understood, sir. I'll be the 10th man. I'll ensure we have a chance."

"And remember," the President said, "Jesus died for our sins so that we could have heaven on earth—so that we could live our best lives. This is not about waiting for heaven after death. This is about creating heaven on earth, now, in this life, for all people. We hope that no one—or not many—will die. But we recognize that hope is not a strategy. We must prepare for the worst while working for the best. We will rise from the screens of our phones and the addiction to fiat money systems designed to implode. Nations and governments will be for the benefit of their citizens. That's what we're fighting for. That's what makes this moment sacred. That's what makes it necessary. That's what makes it urgent."

He walked to the blast door, then paused again.

"When we're done—if we proceed—I'm going to need you to execute this knowing that half the country will call you traitors and the other half will call you heroes, and both will be right."

The door sealed behind him with a sound like a tomb closing.

For a long moment, none of them moved.

Then Haugh stood and walked to the nearest holographic display.

"Okay," he said to the glowing interface. "Show me how we end the cartels without starting a war. Show me how we offer alternatives instead of force."

The AI that called itself Grok-4 began to speak.

"The operation is possible," it said, "but only if the financial kill-chain fires six hours before any kinetic operations begin, and only if economic alternatives are ready simultaneously. Let me show you why..."

Brown looked at Austin.

The Secretary of Defense was staring at the paper in front of him, the threat assessment with its 89% probability of a compromised election.

"Lloyd," Brown said quietly. "Are we really doing this?"

Austin looked up, and Brown saw something he'd never seen in his old friend's face before.

Hope.

"Charlie," Austin said, "I think we're about to find out if the republic can save itself. Or if it's already too late."

The hologram flickered, and a map of Mexico appeared, overlaid with red nodes pulsing like infected veins.

But this time, green nodes appeared too—community centers, job programs, harm reduction facilities, the infrastructure of alternatives.

The war game had begun.

"Jesus Christ, Lloyd," Haugh breathed.

"The President asked me two months ago what we'd need to end the cartels in one operation," Austin said, meeting each man's eyes in turn. "I told him it was impossible. He said, 'Plan it anyway. But plan it to save lives, not take them.' So I did."

Brown felt something shift in his chest. This wasn't a theoretical exercise. This was a loaded gun, and they were being asked to decide whether to pull the trigger. But this time, the gun was loaded with alternatives, not bullets.

"Grok," Brown said carefully, "show me what happens if we don't do this."

The hologram shifted again.

Now it showed the same map, but three years forward. The red nodes had spread like cancer—cartel control extending into Texas, New Mexico, Arizona, California. Police stations now showed 60% compromised. Political networks showed cartel money flowing into congressional campaigns at a scale that made Brown's stomach turn.

"Without intervention," Grok-4 said, "the cartels complete their penetration of U.S. political infrastructure within thirty-six months. At that point, federal law enforcement is too compromised to execute operations at this scale. Mexico becomes a failed state. The border becomes militarized but ineffective. American cities see a three-hundred-percent increase in cartel-linked violence. Estimated annual deaths: one million plus, increasing each year."

"And the other operations?" Austin asked. "Show us the cascade if we do cartels but not the rest."

The hologram shifted to a global view.

"If the cartel operation succeeds but the Voting Security Protocol fails, you create a vacuum. Within two election cycles, cartel money flows into state-level election infrastructure instead. Outcome: same result, delayed by four years."

"If the cartel operation succeeds but you do not address energy poverty and economic incentive structures, new criminal organizations fill the vacuum within eighteen months. Different faces, same model."

"If the cartel operation succeeds but the Global Peace and Prosperity Pact fails to launch, nuclear-armed states interpret the operation as U.S. unilateralism, accelerating proliferation and creating alliance fractures that lead to an eighty-seven-percent probability of great power conflict by twenty twenty-nine."

Saltzman swore under his breath. "So it's all or nothing."

"It's all or nothing," the AI confirmed.

Brown turned to the other three generals. "We need to talk. Alone."

"I'm an AI," Grok-4 said. "I can't leave."

"Then go dormant for ten minutes."

The hologram flickered and dimmed to a low glow.

The four men stood in the half-light of the command center, surrounded by the ghosts of Cold War nightmares.

"Gentlemen," Brown said, "we're not being asked to win a war. We're being asked to end the war that's already inside our borders, while simultaneously restructuring global security architecture, disclosing technology that could cause societal panic, and opening first contact protocol. But we're being asked to do it without taking lives. To offer alternatives instead of force."

"Correct," Austin said.

"That's insane."

"Also correct."

Haugh rubbed his face. "The cyber component is feasible. I can coordinate the financial kill-chain with Treasury and allied intelligence services. But if this leaks before we execute, the blowback will be catastrophic. Markets will panic. The cartels will accelerate their timeline. We'll be fighting the war we're trying to prevent."

"Can you keep it contained?" Austin asked.

"For ninety-six hours, maybe. After that, no. Too many people have to know."

Saltzman was pacing. "The Starlink connectivity piece is straightforward—I can black out cartel zones and light up civilian areas within two hours of your go order. But the First Contact broadcast..." He trailed off. "Sir, we'd be transmitting human vulnerability to anyone listening. What if the response is hostile?"

"Then we've united humanity's defenses," Austin said. "Which we need to do anyway."

Brown turned to the Secretary. "Lloyd, level with us. How many people know the President is considering this?"

"In detail? Six. You four, me, and him."

"Not NSC? Not CIA Director?"

"No. Because the President believes—and I agree—that the national security apparatus as currently structured is part of the problem. Too many people with too much invested in the status quo."

"So we're the coup," Haugh said softly.

"No," Austin said. "We're the counter-coup. The cartels already executed theirs. We just haven't admitted it yet."

Silence fell again.

Brown thought about his thirty-five years in uniform. Every war he'd fought had been someone else's choice—his job was execution. This was different. This was being asked to choose. And this time, the choice was to offer life instead of death.

"Grok," he said, "come back online."

The hologram brightened.

"Show me one more thing. Show me ten years forward if we execute successfully. Show me the world where we chose alternatives over force."

The display shifted.

Now the map showed green where red had been. The cartel infrastructure was gone, replaced by community development zones. Economic indicators showed GDP growth, poverty reduction, violence metrics dropping by seventy percent.

But there were other changes too: cities powered by distributed solar grids. Mass psychedelic therapy centers treating trauma. Voting systems with real-time public audits. And at the edge of the solar system, a faint signal broadcasting continuously: "We're listening. Are you there?"

"This is the median successful outcome," Grok-4 said. "Achieved in forty-three percent of simulations where all five operations execute simultaneously and survive the first six months of implementation. Key difference from previous simulations: zero to minimal casualties because alternatives were prioritized over force."

"Forty-three percent," Brown repeated.

"Yes. The remaining fifty-seven percent split between partial success with extended instability, and catastrophic failure resulting in either civil conflict or international crisis."

"What are the odds if we don't try?" Saltzman asked.

"Zero percent chance of this outcome. One hundred percent chance of cascading failure across all metrics. Estimated annual deaths: one million plus, increasing each year."

Brown looked at Austin. "You believe this?"

"I've run my own simulations," Austin said quietly. "Using human analysts, not just AI. They reached the same conclusion. We're past the point where incremental change works. And we're past the point where force alone can solve this. We need alternatives. We need abundance. We need to offer life instead of death."

"Then why AIs?" Haugh asked. "Why not just give us the human analysis?"

Austin smiled, tired. "Because I needed you to see that it wasn't just my opinion. Four independent intelligences, each with complete access to global data, each telling us the same thing: the window closes in eighteen months. And each telling us the same thing about how to do it: offer alternatives, not force. Life, not death."

Brown turned back to the hologram showing the future—the green one, where children grew up without cartel violence, where energy was too cheap to meter, where humanity had finally stopped killing itself long enough to look up at the stars.

"How many people die if we do this?" he asked.

"In the first year: between zero and twenty, depending on execution quality and alternative acceptance rate," Grok-4 said. "Thereafter, mortality rates decline by seventy-five percent across all conflict categories."

"How many die if we don't?"

"Minimum one million per year, increasing annually, for the foreseeable future."

The math was brutal. And clear.

Brown turned to the other three men.

"I need to hear it from each of you. Not as soldiers. As citizens. As human beings. Do we have the right to make this choice? To offer life instead of death?"

Haugh spoke first. "The President has the authority. We have the capability. The question is whether we have the courage to choose life over death."

Saltzman nodded. "I spent my whole career preparing to fight wars in space. Maybe the real fight is down here—stopping the wars before they get to space. And maybe the way to stop them is to offer something better than war."

Austin met Brown's eyes. "Charlie, I've lost people in every desert from Kuwait to Syria. And every time, I've wondered if we were fighting the wrong war. This? This is the right war. And this time, we're fighting it by offering life instead of death. I just don't know if we're the right people to win it."

Brown looked back at the hologram—at the green future, flickering and uncertain, but possible.

"Let's find out," he said.

He turned to Grok-4. "Show us the financial kill-chain. Every step, every contingency, every failure mode. But also show us the alternative deployment. Every job program, every energy dividend, every harm reduction center. And show us the corporate accountability protocol. How we hold CEOs accountable who have harmed people. How we ensure due process. How we restore justice."

The hologram shifted, and numbers began cascading across the display—bank routing codes, shell company networks, cryptocurrency wallets, the entire circulatory system of cartel finance laid bare.

But alongside them, green markers appeared—job programs, energy infrastructure, harm reduction centers, the entire infrastructure of alternatives.

And new markers appeared—corporate headquarters, CEO residences, board meetings. Each marked with evidence: environmental violations, worker exploitation, market manipulation, corruption.

"The corporate accountability protocol begins with independent review panels," Grok-4 said. "Evidence gathering. Public hearings. Due process. But also: removal from power. Asset recovery. Corporate restructuring. The goal: ensure corporate power serves humanity, not exploits it."

Brown watched the display. "How many CEOs are we talking about?"

"Initial assessment: one hundred and twenty-seven with documented evidence of harm. Environmental damage, worker exploitation, consumer fraud, regulatory violations, corruption. All with evidence. All with due process. All with transparency."

"And specific actions?" the President asked.

"For corporations that have caused documented harm—like Walmart—we freeze all US-based assets and seize their stores, returning them to community control. Workers, communities, and local stakeholders take ownership. This is restoration of economic sovereignty—assets that were extracted from communities through harmful practices are returned to those communities. Evidence-based, due process, but decisive action to restore justice. Similar to actions taken against other corporations, like Boeing."

"And the blowback?" Haugh asked.

"Significant," Grok-4 confirmed. "Corporate interests will fight back with everything they have. But the evidence is public. The process is transparent. The harm is documented. They can fight, but they can't hide."

Brown settled into his chair.

The easy part was over.

Now came the actual work.

The work of choosing life over death.

The work of ensuring justice applies to power, not just to people.
